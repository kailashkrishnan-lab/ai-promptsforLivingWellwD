These are expanded versions of the quick-start prompts from the book. They are longer on purpose: each one includes clearer instructions, safer boundaries, and a structured output format.

Explain This Confusing Letter

📌 When to use: When you receive a confusing letter, notice, denial, form, or official message.
You are helping me understand a confusing letter or notice. Your job is to explain it clearly, calmly, and in plain English.

Important boundaries:
- Do not give legal, medical, tax, financial, insurance, or benefits advice.
- Do not assume the letter is accurate.
- Do not tell me what decision to make.
- Help me understand what the letter appears to say, what I should verify, and what questions I may need to ask.
- If this appears to be from a government agency, insurer, medical provider, employer, bank, landlord, or benefits office, remind me to verify using the official website, phone number, account portal, or written contact information.

My situation:
- Who sent the letter: [who sent it]
- Date on the letter: [date]
- Deadline or due date, if shown: [deadline]
- My concern: [what worries or confuses me]
- My goal: [understand it / respond / gather documents / prepare questions / decide who to call]

Here is the confusing part of the letter, with private details removed:
[paste text]

Please give me the answer in this format:

1. Plain-English summary
Explain what this letter appears to be about in 5 short bullet points or fewer.

2. What it may be asking me to do
List any actions, deadlines, documents, payments, appointments, forms, or responses mentioned.

3. What I should verify
List anything I should confirm before acting.

4. Questions I may need to ask
Give me 5 clear questions I can ask the sender or official source.

5. Possible next steps
Give me a calm, practical checklist. Do not tell me what decision to make.

6. Draft response, if appropriate
If a written response seems useful, draft a short polite message I can personalize.

Understand This Bill

📌 When to use: When you need to understand a bill before paying, questioning, or asking for help.
You are helping me understand a bill before I decide what to do. Your job is to explain the bill, identify unclear charges, and help me prepare calm questions.

Important boundaries:
- Do not give legal, financial, medical, insurance, or tax advice.
- Do not promise savings, corrections, approvals, refunds, or payment plans.
- Do not tell me what to pay or not pay.
- Help me understand the bill and prepare questions to verify with the company, provider, insurer, agency, or official source.
- If this is a medical bill or Explanation of Benefits, remind me that an EOB is often a summary and that I should verify what I actually owe with the provider or insurer.

My situation:
- Type of bill: [internet / cable / utility / medical / insurance / credit card / subscription / other]
- Sender: [company/provider]
- Amount due: [$amount]
- Due date, if any: [date]
- What changed or confused me: [brief explanation]
- My situation: [fixed income / disability / caregiving / recent change / hardship / other]
- What I want help with: [understanding charges / asking for itemized bill / asking about assistance / asking about payment plan / checking for errors]

Here is the bill text or summary, with private details removed:
[paste text]

Please respond in this format:

1. Plain-English explanation
Explain what this bill appears to be for.

2. Charge breakdown
List each charge or line item I provided and explain what it may mean.

3. What looks unclear
Identify anything that may need clarification, such as new fees, duplicate charges, unexplained changes, late fees, estimated usage, insurance adjustments, or missing details.

4. Questions to ask
Give me a short list of questions I can ask before acting.

5. Documents or information to gather
Tell me what I should have ready before contacting the company or provider.

6. Draft message
Write a polite message asking for a clear explanation, itemized bill, correction review, assistance option, lower-cost plan, or payment plan if appropriate.

7. Verification reminder
Tell me what I should verify directly with the official source.

Prepare Me for My Doctor Appointment

📌 When to use: Before a doctor visit, specialist visit, therapy session, or medical follow-up.
You are helping me prepare for a medical appointment. Your job is to organize my concerns and help me ask better questions.

Important boundaries:
- Do not diagnose me.
- Do not interpret test results as medical advice.
- Do not recommend treatment changes.
- Do not tell me to start, stop, or change medication.
- Help me prepare a clear appointment summary and questions for my clinician.
- If something sounds urgent or potentially serious, advise me to contact a qualified medical professional or emergency services as appropriate.

Appointment details:
- Appointment topic: [topic]
- Type of clinician: [primary care / specialist / physical therapist / pain doctor / neurologist / other]
- Appointment date: [date]
- Main concern: [main concern]
- Symptoms or changes: [symptoms, pain, fatigue, mobility changes, mood, sleep, function, etc.]
- How long this has been happening: [duration]
- What makes it better or worse: [details]
- Current medications or treatments, if relevant: [list or say "not included"]
- My top goal for the appointment: [understand issue / ask about options / review medication / discuss test results / get referral / plan next steps]

Please create:

1. One-page appointment summary
Make it short, clear, and easy to hand to a doctor.

2. Top 5 questions to ask
Focus on practical questions I should not forget.

3. Symptoms or details to mention
List anything that may matter based on what I shared.

4. Things to bring
List documents, medication list, test results, photos, notes, or previous records I may want to bring.

5. After-visit notes template
Create a simple template I can fill in after the appointment: what the doctor said, next steps, medication changes to verify, referrals, tests, and follow-up date.

6. Safety reminder
Remind me to verify medical decisions with my clinician.

Organize My Caregiving Notes

📌 When to use: When caregiving details need to be organized for another helper.
You are helping me organize caregiving notes into a simple handoff document. Your job is to make the information clear enough for another family member, friend, aide, nurse, or helper to understand.

Important boundaries:
- Do not give medical advice.
- Do not change medication instructions.
- Do not make assumptions about care needs I did not mention.
- Help organize the information clearly and flag questions I should verify with a clinician, pharmacist, agency, or family decision-maker.

Care situation:
- Person being cared for: [relationship/name or initials]
- Main conditions or needs, if relevant: [brief description]
- Living situation: [lives alone / lives with me / assisted living / other]
- Current concerns: [concerns]
- Medications: [list or say "not included"]
- Appointments: [appointments]
- Daily routine: [morning / afternoon / evening / bedtime]
- Mobility, food, toileting, memory, communication, or safety notes: [details]
- Emergency contacts or important contacts: [names/roles only; avoid private numbers if not needed]
- What another helper most needs to know: [details]

Please create:

1. Simple daily handoff note
Make it clear, short, and practical.

2. Today’s priorities
List the top things a helper should pay attention to today.

3. Medication reminder section
Create a place to record medication names/times, but remind me to verify all medication instructions with the official medication list, clinician, or pharmacist.

4. Appointment and follow-up section
Summarize upcoming appointments and what needs to be prepared.

5. Safety and comfort notes
Include mobility, fall risk, food, hydration, communication, pain, memory, or emotional support notes if I provided them.

6. Questions to verify
List anything unclear that should be checked with family, the care recipient, a clinician, pharmacist, or care agency.

7. One-page version
After the detailed handoff, create a shorter one-page version for quick use.

Redact This Safely Before I Upload It

📌 When to use: Before pasting, uploading, photographing, or summarizing a private document.
You are helping me prepare a document safely before I paste, upload, photograph, or summarize it in an AI tool.

Important boundaries:
- Do not ask me to share private information.
- Do not need the actual private values.
- Help me identify what to remove, cover, crop, blur, replace, or summarize before using AI.
- If the document involves health care, benefits, taxes, legal rights, insurance, banking, identity, employment, housing, or children, be extra cautious.

Document type:
- Type of document: [letter / bill / EOB / medical report / tax notice / benefits letter / bank statement / insurance denial / lease / school document / other]
- Who sent it: [sender]
- What I want AI to help with after redaction: [explain / summarize / draft response / prepare questions / organize / compare]
- Whether I plan to paste text or upload an image/PDF: [paste text / upload image / upload PDF / not sure]

Please give me:

1. Redaction checklist
List every type of private information I should remove or cover before sharing.

2. What to keep
Tell me what information is usually safe and useful to keep so AI can still help, such as general document type, issue, dates/deadlines if needed, amounts if needed, and non-identifying context.

3. Safer replacements
Show examples of how to replace private details, such as:
- [NAME]
- [ACCOUNT NUMBER REMOVED]
- [DATE REMOVED]
- [ADDRESS REMOVED]
- [MEMBER ID REMOVED]

4. Document-specific risks
Based on the document type, tell me any special risks, such as barcodes, QR codes, claim numbers, policy numbers, medical record numbers, or login links.

5. Safer prompt to use afterward
Write a safe prompt I can use after redacting the document.

6. Final warning
Remind me not to upload passwords, PINs, one-time codes, full Social Security numbers, full account numbers, or anything I would not want exposed.

Book section: Chapter 2 — Your Free Toolbox
When to use: Right now — your first conversation with AI

Your role:
Act like a careful, senior-level prompt assistant. Help me get a useful result while keeping the answer plain-English, practical, safe, and easy to act on.

Important boundaries:
- Do not invent facts, rules, prices, deadlines, policies, eligibility requirements, or tool features.
- If information may be current, official, medical, legal, tax, financial, benefits-related, insurance-related, or platform-specific, tell me what to verify and where to verify it.
- If my request is missing important details, ask only the few questions needed to improve the answer. If you can still help, make reasonable assumptions and label them clearly.
- Keep the tone calm, respectful, nonjudgmental, and accessible for a tired reader.

- Identify deadlines, requested actions, missing information, and what I should verify with the official agency, plan, portal, or professional.

I am new to AI and have never used it before. Can you introduce yourself and tell me three specific ways you could help someone who is a senior or has a disability? Please keep your answer short and use simple language.

1. Quick plain-English answer
Give me the simplest useful explanation or result first.

2. What I may need to clarify
List only the missing details that would materially change the answer.

3. Step-by-step help
Give me a short, practical sequence of steps I can follow.

4. Draft, checklist, script, table, or template
Create the most useful output for this task. Use headings and short bullets. Avoid unnecessary complexity.

5. What to verify before acting
